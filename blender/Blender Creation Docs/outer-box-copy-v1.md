# Outer Amazon Box — Complete Copy

**Last Updated:** March 16, 2026
**Status:** Draft v1 — Full copy for all 6 surfaces + interior
**Purpose:** Every text element on the outer Amazon box, organized by surface in design priority order.
**Voice:** 100% Corporate Nemesis (Brad Sterling). Earnest, polished, zero self-awareness. He is never in on the joke.
**Brand:** Elevate Co.
**Product:** Office Survival Kit — Professional Edition™
**Nemesis:** Brad Sterling (B.S.) — Chief Vision Officer & Founder, Elevate Co.
**URL:** bradsterling.com (real page — "LAUNCHING SOON" with headshot and newsletter signup)

> **The 80/20 Rule:** 80% convincing, 20% absurd. A skimming shopper should think this is a real product for 3-5 seconds. A close reader should start laughing. The nemesis NEVER winks.

> **Reference:** For physical specs, surface dimensions, and FBA compliance zones, see amazon-strategy-v1.md. For the briefcase exterior copy (different voice, different surface), see exterior-copy-v4.md.

## SURFACE 1 — FRONT FACE (15.5" × 6.5")

*This IS the Amazon Slot 1 main image. It appears as the primary visible surface in the 3/4 angle product photo. Must work at three scales: thumbnail (150×150px), arm's length (gift moment), and zoom (read the copy). Layout is a wide panoramic strip — think the narrow front edge of a board game box.*

---

### OB-1: Brand Mark
**Treatment:** Clean corporate wordmark, top-center of the surface. Understated. The brand sits ABOVE the product name to establish hierarchy — Elevate Co. is the parent, the kit is just one product in the (imaginary) portfolio.

**Placement:** Top center, small relative to product name

> **ELEVATE CO.**
> ————
> Workplace Solutions Division

**Design note:** The "Workplace Solutions Division" sub-line implies Elevate Co. has multiple divisions. It doesn't. The divider line between the brand and the division name should feel like a real corporate brand lockup — clean, geometric, possibly with a small logomark (a minimalist upward arrow or abstract "E" — designer's call).

---

### OB-2: Product Name
**Treatment:** The hero text element. Large, bold, dominant — this must be readable at thumbnail size in the Amazon Slot 1 image. Clean sans-serif, corporate. This is the single most important piece of copy on the entire product.

**Placement:** Center of surface, vertically centered, occupying the largest visual footprint

> **OFFICE SURVIVAL KIT**
> Professional Edition™

**Design note:** "OFFICE SURVIVAL KIT" is the primary line — large, bold, potentially all-caps. "Professional Edition™" sits below it in a lighter weight or slightly smaller size, like a product tier designation. The ™ is critical — the nemesis trademarks everything. At thumbnail, "OFFICE SURVIVAL KIT" must be the first thing a human eye reads.

---

### OB-3: Tagline
**Treatment:** Single-line value proposition in nemesis voice. Sits below the product name. Smaller text, but readable at arm's length.

**Placement:** Below product name, centered

> Everything your workplace needs. Nothing your workplace expects.™

**Design note:** This reads as a real tagline for ~4 seconds. Then you realize "nothing your workplace expects" is literally true — nobody expects a briefcase full of fake tattoos and a missing stapler. The ™ on the tagline is the nemesis trademarking a sentence.

---

### OB-4: Component Count Badge
**Treatment:** A clean graphic callout — pill-shaped badge, corner burst, or minimal icon cluster. The kind of "7 PIECES!" callout you see on puzzle boxes and gift sets. Functional: tells the Amazon shopper "this isn't one item, it's a set."

**Placement:** Lower-left area of the front face

> **7** PREMIUM COMPONENTS
> BRIEFCASE INCLUDED

**Design note:** "7 PREMIUM COMPONENTS" is the functional Amazon conversion copy — it communicates value-per-dollar. "BRIEFCASE INCLUDED" is doing double duty: it's a real feature (the briefcase IS included), but it's also absurd because the briefcase is the packaging itself. The nemesis counted the container as a component.

---

### OB-5: Award Badge
**Treatment:** Gold/silver foil-effect circular seal — the kind of "WINNER" sticker you see on book covers and wine bottles. Should look like it was applied after printing, even though it's printed on. Embossed effect if production allows.

**Placement:** Lower-right area of the front face, slightly angled (5°)

> ★ WINNER ★
> International Workplace
> Innovation Awards
> — 2024 —

**Design note:** The International Workplace Innovation Awards do not exist. The seal should look completely real — gold metallic, serif font, star accents. At thumbnail, it reads as "award-winning product." At zoom, you might Google it. You will find nothing. The 2024 date makes it feel current. The nemesis absolutely submitted his own product to an award he invented.

---

### OB-6: Hero Image Zone
**Treatment:** This is a design/photography direction note, not copy. Describes what visual the front face copy is built around.

**Placement:** Right-center or center of the surface, integrated with the product name stack

> **VISUAL DIRECTION:** A glamour shot of the closed briefcase at a slight angle — sleek, moody, premium. The duct tape handle should be visible but lit in a way that reads as "textured industrial-chic grip" rather than "someone ran out of options." The briefcase looks aspirational. It should NOT look funny in this image — the humor comes later. Think luxury luggage catalog lighting applied to matte black cardboard.

**Design note:** The nemesis genuinely believes the briefcase is beautiful. The photo should reflect his delusion. If the duct tape handle is too conspicuous, shoot the angle to minimize it — but don't hide it completely. It should be there for the close reader to notice and think "wait, is that duct tape?"

## SURFACE 3 — BACK FACE (15.5" × 6.5")

*The "read more" surface. Same narrow panoramic proportions as the front face. Visible in Slot 8 detail shots and video. This is where the nemesis drops testimonials, his bio, and the fine print. Higher density than the front — this surface rewards close reading.*

---

### OB-13: Testimonials
**Treatment:** 4 fabricated testimonials in the style of Amazon review pull-quotes or back-of-book blurbs. Each has a name, title, and a quote that reads as plausible for 3-5 seconds, then lands as absurd. Arranged horizontally across the surface with small decorative quotation marks.

**Placement:** Upper half of the surface, spanning the full width

**Testimonial 1:**
> "Changed the way I think about Mondays."
> — D. Martinez, Regional Accounts

**Testimonial 2:**
> "I gave this to my entire team. Productivity is down. Morale is... complicated. Would buy again."
> — J. Adebayo, Operations Lead

**Testimonial 3:**
> "My coworker won't stop reading the cards out loud. I have spoken to management. They are also reading the cards out loud."
> — S. Park, Senior Analyst

**Testimonial 4:**
> "I keep the briefcase on my desk. People ask what it is. I tell them it's classified. I have never felt more powerful."
> — R. Walsh, IT Support

**Design note:** Testimonial 1 is the anchor — it reads as completely real. Could be on any product. Testimonial 2 introduces the first crack ("morale is... complicated" and "would buy again" despite describing a problem). Testimonial 3 is the escalation — describes the product causing an office incident, but the reviewer isn't complaining, just reporting. Testimonial 4 is the payoff — the product has given someone a personality. Each testimonial should have a different visual treatment (different quotation mark styles, slightly different text sizes) to look like they were collected from different sources. Star ratings (★★★★★) optional but effective.

---

### OB-14: Founder Bio
**Treatment:** A 3-4 sentence "About the Founder" or "A Note from Our Team" blurb. Corporate boilerplate applied to someone who clearly doesn't deserve it. Small headshot silhouette placeholder.

**Placement:** Center of the surface, below testimonials

> **ABOUT THE FOUNDER**
>
> When Elevate Co. founder Brad Sterling discovered a crude, unlicensed office survival kit circulating in American cubicle farms, he didn't see contraband. He saw potential. "The original had heart," he says. "What it lacked was branding, individual packaging, trademark symbols, and someone willing to take full credit."
>
> Today, the Office Survival Kit — Professional Edition™ represents the pinnacle of his vision: premium workplace tools, premium packaging, and a premium price point that reflects the premium nature of the premium experience.
>
> Brad Sterling is a three-time nominee for the International Workplace Innovation Awards and an occasional keynote speaker at conferences he organizes himself.

**Design note:** The nemesis openly admits he found someone else's creation, lists "trademark symbols" as one of his improvements, and defines "taking full credit" as a business skill. The word "premium" appears five times in one sentence — intentionally. His bio ends with credentials that undercut themselves: "three-time nominee" (never won — but the front face award badge says WINNER, which means he gave himself the award between writing the bio and printing the box), and "keynote speaker at conferences he organizes himself." Headshot: a stock photo presented as Brad's actual headshot — arms-crossed, slight smile, gray blazer. The kind of "Meet Our Team" photo that's clearly purchased. Small credit beneath: "PHOTO COURTESY OF ELEVATE CO."

### OB-20: Letter from the Founder
**Treatment:** A printed "letter" — the kind of welcome insert you find in premium subscription boxes, but printed directly on the inside lid instead of on a separate card. Formal business letter layout. Nemesis letterhead. His most sustained piece of writing on the product.

**Placement:** Centered on the inside lid, formatted as a formal letter with margins

> ───────────────────────────────
> **ELEVATE CO.**
> Workplace Solutions Division
> ───────────────────────────────
>
> **A Letter from the Founder**
>
> Dear Valued Professional,
>
> Congratulations on your purchase of the Elevate Co. Office Survival Kit — Professional Edition™. Or, if this was given to you by a colleague, congratulations on being the kind of person someone thought was worth it. That says something about you. It also says something about this product.
>
> I designed every detail of what you are about to open. The briefcase. The components. The Multi-Layer Reveal System™ that guides you through the unboxing experience in a sequence I personally engineered. Even the handle — which features a proprietary Reinforced Grip System™ using a material I sourced specifically for its durability and tactile authenticity. You will understand when you feel it.
>
> Inside, you will find seven individually packaged components, each one named, branded, and trademarked by Elevate Co. There is also a hidden compartment. I will not tell you where it is or what is inside it. I will tell you that it is ceramic and that you are not ready.
>
> I encourage you to take your time. The unboxing was designed to be discovered in layers — each one more premium than the last. Do not rush. Do not skip ahead. The experience is the product. The product is the experience. I wrote that sentence twice on purpose.
>
> This is the Office Survival Kit — Professional Edition™. I built it for people who understand that surviving the workplace is not enough. You have to elevate.
>
> You're welcome.
>
> Warmly,
>
> *[Signature]*
> **Brad Sterling**
> Chief Vision Officer & Founder
> Elevate Co.
>
> P.S. — If you find any handwritten notes inside that appear to be from someone who is not me, please disregard them. We are aware of the issue and are working on it.

**Design note:** This is the single most important piece of nemesis copy in the entire product. Every line reveals character:

- "Congratulations on being the kind of person someone thought was worth it" — Brad wants to compliment both the buyer and himself in one sentence. He almost says "worth the money" but catches himself — a premium brand doesn't mention cost.
- "Proprietary Reinforced Grip System™" — Brad is describing duct tape. He trademarked it. When the recipient opens the box and sees duct tape wrapped around cardboard, this sentence becomes retroactively hilarious.
- "I sourced specifically for its durability and tactile authenticity" — Brad is claiming he chose duct tape on purpose. "Tactile authenticity" is the kind of phrase that means absolutely nothing but sounds like it was in a pitch deck.
- "It is ceramic and that you are not ready" — Brad thinks withholding information about a mug creates anticipation. He is treating a mug like a Marvel post-credits scene.
- "I wrote that sentence twice on purpose" — Brad acknowledging his own writing technique in a welcome letter. He thinks this is clever. It is not.
- "Surviving the workplace is not enough. You have to elevate." — Brad's personal philosophy and his company name collide in a sentence he is extremely proud of. He has said this at conferences.
- The P.S. is the bridge to the interior: he knows the OG's sticky notes are inside. He can't remove them (they're printed on the boxes). He's asking the customer to ignore the other voice. This is the setup for the OG/nemesis collision the recipient is about to discover.

**This letter should be the last thing read before opening the briefcase.** The next thing the recipient sees is matte black cardboard, duct tape, and "CLEARED FOR OFFICE USE (BARELY)." The whiplash is the product — Brad just called that duct tape a "Proprietary Reinforced Grip System™" and now you're looking right at it.
