## STRATEGIC IDENTITY

### Purpose Statement
Brad Sterling is the reason this kit exists as a product. He found OG's original contraband survival manual, saw a business opportunity, and built Elevate Co. around it. He designed all packaging (outer Amazon box AND the briefcase exterior), inserted his own branding wherever he could monetize, renamed the items, and sells it at a markup. He is the corporate appropriation layer — the guy who turns authentic grassroots culture into a premium SKU.

### Narrative Function
Brad is the in-world answer to "why does a handmade contraband manual come in premium packaging?" He designed the exterior. He renamed the items. He added his own elements inside. He thinks he improved it. He didn't. And OG got to this specific copy before it shipped — sticky notes, handwritten annotations, the real voice bleeding through Brad's corporate veneer like graffiti on a billboard.

| Briefcase exterior | Brad's design + sparse OG bleed-through | Brad's "premium packaging" with OG's guerrilla marks. First cracks in Brad's veneer. |

Brad's rebranded item boxes sit in OG's recesses — crossed out and corrected by OG. Brad took credit for the layout ("Multi-Layer Reveal System™") but the design is unmistakably OG's. |

**The name journey the recipient takes:**
1. Outer box → sees "Executive Tactical Stylus™" and Brad's other rebrands. Thinks those are the item names.
2. Opens briefcase → sees OG's chalk outlines with "Cubicle Shank" in the tray. Realizes there are different names — original names.
3. Picks up item box → sees Brad's printed name crossed out, OG's handwritten correction beside it. The whole story clicks: OG built this, Brad repackaged it, OG came back and set the record straight.

### Unique Contribution
Brad is the only character who doesn't understand what he's selling. He's never survived an office — he's an "entrepreneur." He commodified something authentic without understanding why it was authentic. Everything about his presence confirms that the contraband version was better.

### The Lens
Brad sees the world as a personal branding opportunity. Every surface is a billboard. Every interaction is content. He speaks the office-as-prison vocabulary fluently — it's the language of his market and he deploys it with precision in his product copy. But he's never actually done time. He's an entrepreneur who's always been on the outside. He's the guy who opens a military surplus store without having served. He knows the language like a marketer, not a veteran. That gap — fluent but inexperienced — is what separates Brad's voice from OG's even when they use the same vocabulary.

## HOW BRAD'S COMEDY WORKS

### One Voice, Three Situations

Brad always sounds like Brad: polished, earnest, hustle-culture cadence, totally sincere, never in on the joke. That voice is constant. What changes is what he's talking about — and some subjects expose him more than others.

**When Brad talks about the office/product:** His competence shows. He knows his audience and he can land a joke inside a straight-faced format. This is why Elevate Co. exists — the product copy actually works.

**When Brad talks about himself:** His delusion shows. The same competent marketer who wrote the Nutrition Facts parody will, in the very next element, art-direct a gold-framed headshot of himself in a purple satin shirt and stack the word "premium" five times in one sentence. He sees no contradiction.

**When Brad talks about how he built the business:** His obliviousness shows. He openly admits he found someone else's work, added only cosmetic changes, gave himself awards, and can't control the OG — and frames every bit of it as a success story. "What it lacked was branding, individual packaging, trademark symbols, and someone willing to take full credit."

These aren't three different Brads. They're three windows into the same guy. The reader should never sense a voice shift — they should just keep laughing and not be able to articulate exactly why some lines are funny-clever and some are funny-delusional. It all just sounds like Brad.

### The Comedy Gradient

What makes Brad work is that his self-awareness slides on a spectrum depending on the subject:

| Brad is talking about... | Self-awareness level | Comedy type |
|--------------------------|---------------------|-------------|
| Office life / the product | High — he knows what's funny | Genuinely good jokes in straight-faced formats |
| Himself / his brand | Zero — complete delusion | The gap between his self-image and reality |
| How he built the business | Negative — he confesses without knowing it | Accidental honesty framed as achievement |

The gradient is what makes him feel like a real person. Everyone has subjects they're sharp about and subjects where they're blind. Brad is sharp about office humor and blind about himself. That's not three characters — that's one very specific, very real human being.

### Why the Product Copy Should Be Good

Don't sabotage Brad's office humor to make him look dumber. The Nutrition Facts label should be genuinely great. The IT Asset Tag should land. Brad CAN do this. The better his product copy is, the funnier it becomes when he pivots to talking about himself — because the reader thinks "this guy clearly has a brain, so HOW does he not see what he looks like?" If everything were bad, he'd just be incompetent. The fact that he's good at the job and blind about himself is what makes him feel real.

## BRAD'S DESIGN AESTHETIC

### What Brad Did to the Product
Brad found OG's grassroots kit and made the following "improvements" (all cosmetic):
- Designed the outer Amazon box (100% Brad's marketing)
- Designed the briefcase exterior (matte black, "premium" — Brad thinks this screams "executive")
- Renamed every item (OG's originals → Brad's trademarked versions)
- Added individual item packaging
- Added trademark symbols to everything
- Added award badges (self-given)
- Added himself (About the Founder panels, welcome letter, headshots)
- Added the "Multi-Layer Reveal System™" (a trademarked name for "things are inside other things")

### What Brad Did NOT Change
- The actual items (OG's originals, just repackaged)
- The booklets (OG's content, untouched)
- The briefcase interior layout (OG's design — chalk outlines, tray system, Pelican-case recesses, reveal sequence — all OG's work)
- The sticky notes and handwritten annotations (OG broke in after Brad's version was printed)

### What Brad Took Credit For Anyway
- The interior reveal sequence — Brad calls it the "Multi-Layer Reveal System™" and presents it as his innovation. In the welcome letter: "I encourage you to take your time with the unboxing experience. It was engineered to be discovered in layers." Engineered by OG, trademarked by Brad.

**The visual:** On every item box inside the kit, you see Brad's printed name crossed out with OG's handwritten original beside it. Same pattern as the QR code layer cake — Brad prints something official, OG defaces it with the truth. Repeatable across every item.

## THE COMEDY RULE

> **Format stays buttoned-up. Content gets unleashed.**

The typography, layout, label designs, FDA formatting, corporate letterhead, award seals — all that stays polished and straight-faced. That's the straight man. The actual words inside those formats go for laughs on every line. Not one joke per element. Jokes stacked on jokes, escalating, rewarding re-reads.

The format credibility is what *makes* the content funny. Without it, the jokes have no frame. But the content itself should never play it safe.

**The question to ask when writing any Brad copy:**
> "How funny can every line be while the format stays perfectly straight?"

**What this means in practice:**
- Every line is an opportunity for a joke, a callback, a confessional drop, or a detail that rewards close reading
- The format (label style, typography, layout, official structure) never breaks character
- Density is a feature — the reader who scans should laugh, the reader who reads closely should laugh harder, the reader who re-reads should find things they missed
- This is a gag gift. The exterior box must grab attention and keep people laughing. Every surface earns its space by being funny.

**What this replaces:**
The old "80/20 rule" (80% convincing, 20% absurd) optimized for stealth — making the product look real for 3-5 seconds. That's the wrong goal. Nobody buys a gag gift because they were fooled. They buy it because they laughed. The straight-faced format provides all the "realness" needed. The content provides the laughs.

## WRITING BRAD: PRACTICAL PATTERNS

### The Format/Content Escalation
1. First line reads as completely plausible
2. Second line has a slight edge
3. Third line is clearly a joke but the format stays serious
4. Final line is full absurd, format still intact

### The Confessional Drop
Brad buries self-incriminating admissions in corporate language. He thinks they make him sound transparent and visionary. They make him sound like he's testifying.
- Pattern: "[Corporate framing] + [damning admission phrased as achievement]"
- Example: "What it lacked was branding, individual packaging, trademark symbols, and someone willing to take full credit."

### The Credential Stack
Start almost-real, end obviously self-generated, present all with equal weight.
- Pattern: "[Plausible title], [slightly suspect honor], [self-undermining detail played straight]"
- Example: "Three-time nominee... and an occasional keynote speaker at conferences he organizes himself."

### The Trademark Reflex
Brad trademarks everything. Sentences. Product features. The unboxing experience. Things that cannot be trademarked. The ™ symbol is Brad's nervous tic — he puts it on everything because he thinks it makes things official.

### The Premium Stack
Brad uses "premium" the way other people use commas. When he runs out of adjectives, he just says premium again.
- Peak example: "Premium workplace tools, premium packaging, and a premium price point that reflects the premium nature of the premium experience."
