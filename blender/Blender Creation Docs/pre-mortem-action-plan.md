# Pre-Mortem Action Plan: Office Survival Kit

**Date:** March 11, 2026

---

## How to Use This Plan

This action plan contains specific, concrete steps prioritized by urgency and impact. Focus first on "Critical — Do Immediately" items before proceeding with your initiative. Each action is tied to a specific failure scenario from the Pre-Mortem Analysis.

---

## Critical Actions — Do Immediately

**These must be addressed before the sourcing handoff. Failure to act on these creates unacceptable risk.**

---

### Action 1: Create the Dimensions Spec

**Why this matters:** Every other document references it. Your own roadmap flags it as critical priority. It's the #1 item on your "Quick Start: What to Create Next" list. Without it, sourcing has no stable reference point for quoting — they'll be pulling numbers from across 14 different files.

**What to do:**
1. Pull all measurements from the Authority Block into a standalone Dimensions Spec document
2. Mark every value as LOCKED or WORKING ESTIMATE
3. For every WORKING ESTIMATE, add an acceptable tolerance range (e.g., "mug diameter: 3.5" ±0.25"")
4. Include the full stack math for both zones
5. Include the coordinate system reference for Blender work

**Who:** You
**By when:** Before sourcing handoff (this week)
**Resources needed:** Authority Block (already written — this is mostly reorganization)
**Success looks like:** A single document where sourcing can find every measurement without cross-referencing anything else

**Prevents:** Scenario 5 (Documentation Cascade), Scenario 12 (Fragile Handoff)

---

### Action 2: Create a Sourcing Handoff Package

**Why this matters:** Your sourcing coworker needs to understand this product in 15 minutes, not 3 hours. The current 14-document system was built for AI chat sessions. A human needs a different entry point.

**What to do:**
1. Create a single "Sourcing Brief" document (1.5 hours max) containing:
   - 1-paragraph product overview
   - Photo/render of the product concept (even a rough one)
   - Complete item list with dimensions, materials, and key sourcing questions (pull from Item Specs summary table)
   - Manufactured packaging components list (booklets, foam, booking frame, compartment cover, belly band) with specs
   - Box dimensions and critical constraints (FBA size tier, mug as controlling dimension)
   - The 11 sourcing action items from the Authority Block
   - The 17 sourcing questions from the Dimensions Spec
   - Target COGS ceiling ($7-10 per kit) stated explicitly
   - Clear indication of what's LOCKED vs. what's FLEXIBLE
2. Include a "What I need back from you" section: per-item quotes, MOQs, lead times, feasibility flags, assembly cost estimate
3. Attach the Item Specs and Dimensions Spec as reference appendices — but the Brief should be self-sufficient

**Who:** You
**By when:** Immediately after Dimensions Spec is done
**Resources needed:** Authority Block, Item Specs v3, Dimensions Spec (once created)
**Success looks like:** Your sourcing coworker reads the brief, understands the product, and can start quoting within a day without asking more than 2-3 clarifying questions

**Prevents:** Scenario 12 (Fragile Handoff), Scenario 4 (Timeline Slip)

---

### Action 3: Build a COGS Tier Model

**Why this matters:** You're about to negotiate with reality. Going in without knowing your walk-away points means making reactive cuts under pressure. 10 minutes of planning now saves weeks of back-and-forth later.

**What to do:**
1. Create a simple spreadsheet with three columns: Ideal ($10 COGS), Acceptable ($8.50 COGS), Minimum Viable ($7 COGS)
2. For each tier, list:
   - Which items stay
   - Which items get cut or downgraded
   - Which packaging components stay
   - What replaces cut items (copy? simpler alternatives?)
3. Identify your non-negotiables (the features you won't cut at any tier) — probably: the mug, the briefcase form factor, the 2-tray + hidden compartment structure, the booking frame
4. Identify your first-to-cut list (features that add cost but aren't essential) — probably: die-cut foam (replace with cardboard divider), custom-shape eraser (use standard + wrapper), coil binding (use saddle-stitch), belly band (use shrink wrap)
5. Share this with your sourcing coworker alongside the Sourcing Brief so he knows where you have flexibility

**Who:** You
**By when:** Before sourcing handoff
**Resources needed:** Item Specs (for rough cost estimates), a spreadsheet
**Success looks like:** When sourcing quotes come back, you can immediately map them to a tier and make decisions without agonizing

**Prevents:** Scenario 1 (Death by a Thousand Cuts), Scenario 2 (Mug Ate the Budget)

---

### Action 4: Confirm Mug Dimensions with a Physical Sample

**Why this matters:** The mug is the controlling constraint. Its diameter sets the box depth. Its cost sets the COGS floor. Every working estimate in the entire project flows from the mug spec. And right now, the mug dimensions are a guess. A $5 Amazon purchase today could save you a documentation cascade later.

**What to do:**
1. Order 2-3 different 11oz enamel camp mugs from Amazon right now (different brands, $8-15 each)
2. When they arrive, measure: outer diameter, height, handle protrusion, weight
3. Compare to your 3.5" × 3.5" working estimate
4. If dimensions differ, update the Authority Block and Dimensions Spec BEFORE sourcing handoff

**Who:** You
**By when:** Order today; measure when they arrive (2-3 days)
**Resources needed:** ~$30 for sample mugs
**Success looks like:** You know the actual dimensions of a standard 11oz enamel camp mug and can lock the number before it cascades

**Prevents:** Scenario 2 (Mug Ate the Budget), Scenario 5 (Documentation Cascade)

## High Priority — Complete Before or During Sourcing

**These significantly reduce risk and should be completed in the next 2-3 weeks.**

---

### Action 5: Write Copy for Items 6-12

**Why this matters:** 7 of 12 items have TBD copy. Your sourcing coworker doesn't need final copy to quote, but *you* need it to understand whether the product works as a complete set. Writing the copy forces you to ask: "Is this item earning its place in the kit, or is it filler?" That question matters before sourcing, not after.

**What to do:**
1. Write SHORT, MEDIUM, and LONG copy for: Solitary, Ankle Monitor, Evidence Eraser, Mug Shot, Rap Sheet, Corporate Parole Passport, Yard Pass
2. Write tray recess taglines for all 11 tray items (these are the shortest and hardest — 3-5 words each)
3. Test for "joke fatigue" — read all 14 items' copy back-to-back and check if the prison metaphor feels one-note by item 10

**Who:** You
**By when:** Within 2 weeks
**Resources needed:** OG character material (150 pages), approved copy for items 1-5 as voice reference
**Success looks like:** Every item has at least draft copy; the full set reads as varied and escalating, not repetitive

**Prevents:** Scenario 8 (Copy Doesn't Land)

---

### Action 6: Do a Quick Competitive Scan

**Why this matters:** You need to validate the $30 price point before sourcing optimizes toward it. If the market ceiling is $25, you need to know now — it changes your COGS target and your feature priorities.

**What to do:**
1. Search Amazon for "office gag gift," "office survival kit," "funny office gift," "coworker gift set"
2. Note the top 10 results: price, item count, review count, star rating, what's included
3. Look specifically at the $25-35 range: what do products at that price include? How do reviews assess value?
4. Check if Witty Yeti's existing products (Shart Kit, etc.) provide any pricing data
5. Write a 1-page summary: price range, item count norms, review themes, and where Office Survival Kit fits

**Who:** You (1-2 hours)
**By when:** Before sourcing handoff if possible; within 2 weeks at latest
**Resources needed:** Amazon search, 1-2 hours
**Success looks like:** You know whether $30 is in the sweet spot, at the ceiling, or above market expectations

**Prevents:** Scenario 6 (Competitive Pressure Compresses Price)

---

### Action 7: Define Assembly Requirements

**Why this matters:** Assembly cost is a hidden COGS line item. If you don't define it, the manufacturer will interpret it however is easiest for them — which may not match your reveal sequence.

**What to do:**
1. Create a simple 1-page visual assembly guide: numbered steps with a diagram showing what goes where, in what order
2. Flag the mug orientation specifically (on its side, handle to the side) — this is the most error-prone step
3. Ask your sourcing coworker to get an assembly cost estimate alongside component quotes
4. Think about simplification: could any items be pre-packed in sub-assemblies? (e.g., all Tray 2 items in a pre-loaded tray)

**Who:** You (30 minutes for the guide; sourcing gets the quote)
**By when:** Include in sourcing handoff package
**Resources needed:** Reveal sequence from Authority Block or Packaging Spec
**Success looks like:** Manufacturer knows exactly how to pack each kit; assembly cost is a known number in the COGS model

**Prevents:** Scenario 3 (Assembly Nightmare)

### Action 8: Establish Go/No-Go Gates

**Why this matters:** Without decision points, the project drifts. You need clear dates where you assess: "Are we on track for Q4, or do we need to adjust?"

**What to do:**
1. Set three gates:
   - **Gate 1 (April 15):** Sourcing quotes received. COGS model populated. Go/no-go on current item list.
   - **Gate 2 (June 1):** Samples approved. Design finalized. Copy complete. Go/no-go on Q4 timeline.
   - **Gate 3 (August 1):** Production ordered. Photography planned. Listing drafted. Go/no-go on October FBA inbound.
2. At each gate, ask: "What would need to be true for us to proceed?" and "What would cause us to stop or delay?"
3. Share these gates with your sourcing coworker so you're both working toward the same milestones

**Who:** You + sourcing coworker
**By when:** Set the gates now; execute at each date
**Resources needed:** Calendar, 15-minute conversation with sourcing coworker
**Success looks like:** You know by June 1 whether Q4 is realistic, not by September when it's too late

**Prevents:** Scenario 4 (Timeline Slip)

### Action 9: Plan the Photography Strategy Early

**Why this matters:** This product's value proposition is experiential. If the Amazon listing can't convey the unboxing journey, conversion will suffer regardless of how good the product is.

**What to do:**
1. Study how existing "kit" products on Amazon handle photography (especially premium ones like craft cocktail kits, survival kits, or Pelican case products)
2. Draft a rough shot list: closed case hero, open case overhead, tray detail, mug compartment reveal, booking frame in use, item spread
3. Decide early: will you do a short unboxing video? (Strongly recommended for this product)
4. Consider whether the Blender 3D model can generate pre-launch listing images while you wait for physical samples

**Who:** You
**By when:** Before samples arrive (so you can photograph samples when they do)
**Resources needed:** Amazon research, existing Blender model
**Success looks like:** When samples arrive, you already know exactly what shots to take

**Prevents:** Scenario 9 (Photo Problem)

### Action 10: Build Tolerance Ranges Into All Working Estimates

**Why this matters:** The documentation currently uses exact numbers for working estimates (e.g., "3.5" diameter"). When sourcing comes back with 3.625" or 3.75", you need to know instantly whether that's acceptable or whether it triggers a cascade.

**What to do:**
1. For every WORKING ESTIMATE in the Dimensions Spec, add an acceptable range
2. Key tolerances to define:
   - Mug diameter: 3.5" ±? (how much before box depth must change?)
   - Case depth: 5.0" — what's the max before FBA tier changes?
   - Tray footprint: how much can it shrink before items don't fit?
   - Booklet dimensions: what's the minimum for the reveal cover effect?
3. Document the cascade triggers: "If mug diameter exceeds X, then case depth must increase to Y, which changes Z"

**Who:** You
**By when:** When creating the Dimensions Spec (Action 1)
**Resources needed:** Current working estimates + FBA size tier limits
**Success looks like:** Sourcing feedback can be evaluated against tolerances instantly, without re-deriving the math

**Prevents:** Scenario 5 (Documentation Cascade)

## Ongoing Monitoring — Establish Systems

**These are continuous practices to maintain throughout execution.**

---

### Action 11: Weekly Sourcing Check-In

**Why this matters:** The sourcing phase is the critical path. Left unmanaged, it can drift for weeks.

**What to do:**
1. Schedule a weekly 15-minute check-in with your sourcing coworker
2. Agenda: What quotes are in? What's still outstanding? Any feasibility concerns? Any dimension surprises?
3. Track in a simple shared document or spreadsheet

**Who:** You + sourcing coworker
**By when:** Start the week after handoff
**Success looks like:** You know the sourcing status at all times and can react to issues within days, not weeks

---

### Action 12: Track COGS in Real Time

**Why this matters:** As quotes come in, you need to see the running total against your ceiling. Don't wait until all quotes are in to discover you're over budget.

**What to do:**
1. Create a simple COGS tracker spreadsheet
2. As each quote arrives, enter it
3. Running total vs. ceiling ($7 / $8.50 / $10 tiers from Action 3)
4. Flag when cumulative quotes cross a tier threshold

**Who:** You
**By when:** Start when first quotes arrive
**Success looks like:** At any point, you can see where you stand against budget and which tier you're tracking toward

## Decision Points

### Go/No-Go Decision

**Recommendation:** Proceed with sourcing handoff — the product concept is strong, the company has launch infrastructure, and the "wish list first, negotiate down" strategy is sound.

**Conditions for proceeding:**
1. Dimensions Spec is created (stable reference for sourcing)
2. Sourcing Brief is created (clear handoff document)
3. COGS tier model is drafted (you know your walk-away points)

**Conditions that would cause a pause:**
- Mug sample reveals dimensions that force case depth above FBA Large Standard Size threshold
- Competitive scan reveals market ceiling well below $25
- Sourcing coworker flags that the complexity is beyond what the manufacturer can handle

### Approach Decisions

**Recommended changes to approach:**
1. Add tolerance ranges to all working estimates — stop treating them as exact numbers
2. Create a sourcing-specific document package rather than handing over the full 14-document set
3. Order physical mug samples immediately — don't wait for sourcing to do this
4. Build the COGS tier model before handing off — know your negotiation points in advance

### Resource Allocation Decisions

**Recommended resource adjustments:**
1. Deprioritize: Blender model rewrite, web demo, image prompts — these can wait until design is finalized post-sourcing
2. Prioritize: Dimensions Spec, Sourcing Brief, COGS model, copy for items 6-12
3. Timebox: Competitive scan — don't let it become a research project; 2 hours max
