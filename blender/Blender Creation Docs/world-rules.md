# Office Survival Kit — World Rules

**Status:** Working document
**Purpose:** The shared reality that all characters and all copy exist inside. Every piece of writing in this product — OG's, Brad's, the items, the packaging — lives in this world.

---

## THE FUNDAMENTAL RULE

**The office is a prison. This is not a metaphor.**

In this universe, nobody thinks it's clever or funny to compare the office to prison. That comparison doesn't exist because there's nothing to compare — the office just IS this way. You serve your sentence. You do your time. You survive your shift. The language of incarceration is the language of corporate life. It's the water everyone swims in.

This is what makes the product work: the reader recognizes their own reality, not a joke about their reality.

## HOW EACH CHARACTER RELATES TO THIS WORLD

### OG — The Veteran

OG has done hard time. OG built the original survival kit from lived experience — the way someone who actually survived something writes a field manual for the next person. OG's use of this world's language carries weight because it's earned. When OG says "never eat the break room leftovers," that's not a bit — that's a lesson learned the hard way.

**OG's relationship to the world:** Insider. Survivor. Authority. OG speaks this language because OG lived it.

### Brad Sterling — The Outsider Who Knows the Language

Brad has never done time. He's an entrepreneur — he's always been on the outside. But he knows this world's language fluently because it's the language of his market. He's studied it. He can deploy it in product copy with precision. He just hasn't *lived* it.

This is why Brad's product copy works — he genuinely understands what office workers relate to. And it's why his personal branding feels off — he's selling survival gear for a world he's never actually survived in. He's the guy who opens a military surplus store without having served.

**Brad's relationship to the world:** Adjacent. Fluent but inexperienced. He speaks the language like a marketer, not a veteran.

### The Reader / Recipient — The Current Inmate

The reader is currently doing time. They're in it right now. The kit is a gift from someone who sees them, or a purchase by someone who sees themselves. The vocabulary of this world isn't something the reader needs explained — they live it every day. That's why the product resonates. It's not teaching them a metaphor. It's naming their reality.

**The reader's relationship to the world:** Active participant. Currently serving. The target audience for OG's wisdom and Brad's marketing alike.

## WHAT THIS MEANS FOR COPY

### Every piece of copy lives in this world
The prison/office vocabulary isn't reserved for "the funny parts." It's the default language for everything — item names, packaging labels, Brad's marketing, OG's annotations, product descriptions. Stepping outside this vocabulary should feel like stepping out of character.

### The world is played straight
Nobody comments on the fact that they're describing an office like a prison. Nobody winks. Nobody says "get it? because the office is like a prison!" The moment anyone acknowledges the comparison as a comparison, the world breaks. It's only funny because everyone treats it as completely normal.

### Absurdity comes from specificity, not from the premise
The premise (office = prison) is the ground floor. It's not where the jokes live. The jokes live in the specific details WITHIN that premise — the specific survival tips, the specific items, the specific ways Brad monetizes it, the specific things OG has seen. The world itself is a given. What happens inside the world is where the comedy is.

### The vocabulary can evolve
This list isn't exhaustive. New terms should emerge naturally from the writing. The test for any new term: would someone in this world use it without thinking twice? If yes, it belongs. If it feels like a reach or a forced joke, it doesn't.

---

## THE WORLD'S TONE

The overall tone of this world is **sardonic, not cynical.** The people in it haven't given up — they've adapted. OG survived and built a manual to help others survive. Brad saw a market and built a business. The reader is getting through it with humor and solidarity. Nobody is nihilistic. Nobody thinks it's hopeless. The attitude is: "this is absurd, we all know it's absurd, and here's how we get through it together."

That warmth underneath the dark humor is what makes it a gift someone gives to a coworker they actually like. It's not mean. It's validating.
