## 1. Concept: The Contraband Manual

The packaging is a makeshift briefcase — an improvised survival manual assembled on the inside and passed down. Not issued from above. Built by someone who survived and wanted to make sure you do too. This is the OG character's artifact.

The contraband manual aesthetic (coil-bound booklets, handmade feel) is a character in the world — it reinforces the duct tape handle, the matte black exterior, the faux clasps. Everything about this object says: *someone made this under difficult conditions because they needed it.*

The exterior reads as a worn, institutional briefcase. The interior reveals a two-zone contraband layout — a front zone with stacked item trays separated by coil-bound booklets, and a back zone hiding the final payoff in a padded compartment. Each layer builds a narrative arc from intake to release. The recipient doesn't just open a box. They get processed.

### Chalk Outline & Die-Cut Recess Style

Every item in every tray sits within a die-cut recess (Pelican case style) — a molded cutout shaped precisely to each item. This is not an open tray with loose items; each item has a fitted pocket that prevents shifting and provides a premium unboxing feel.

Each die-cut recess has a chalk-outline silhouette printed on the tray surface surrounding the cutout, plus the item name and a short tagline printed adjacent to the recess. The chalk outlines serve three purposes: they tell the recipient where each item goes (and that one is "missing" if removed), they reinforce the crime-scene / institutional world, and they provide a clean, premium presentation that reads as intentional rather than cluttered.

Outlines are printed in white or cream on the darker tray floor. Line weight should feel hand-drawn — not perfectly uniform, not digitally precise. Slightly rough edges. Think actual chalk on a dark surface, not vector art.

**Front Zone (facing the person):** Trays 1 and 2 stacked vertically with coil-bound booklets between them. Trays are full interior width (no ring clearance needed) and centered in the front zone.

Completely hidden while trays are in place. Revealed when Tray 2 is lifted and a lift-off cover is removed.

### WARNING / DOCUMENTED CASES (Booklet 1, Page 1)

The first page of Booklet 1 contains a WARNING notice. This sets the tone immediately — bureaucratic language played completely straight, establishing that this "manual" has a documented history of incidents.

Content direction: A formal warning that this survival kit has been associated with documented workplace incidents. Lists 3–5 absurd "cases" in official language. Establishes that this manual is contraband — possession is not officially sanctioned. Tone: dead serious. Format: institutional warning notice.

*Full copy lives in the Copy Document.*

### Sticky Notes (Inside Both Booklets)

- Attached to booklet pages — not loose, not in pockets
- Electronics-grade adhesive — stays put through shipping and handling
- Designed to look like sticky notes — color, shape, lined or unlined
- Content: OG character's handwritten annotations, warnings, survival tips
- These are the primary mechanism for the OG character's voice showing up throughout the pages
- The handwriting is what makes it feel personal, contraband, passed-down

### Polaroid Cards (Split Across Both Booklets)

- Polaroid-style printed cards — white border, slight aging
- Split across both booklets (some in Booklet 1, some in Booklet 2)
- Inserted loose between pages or housed in a pocket/sleeve within each booklet
- Content TBD pending OG character details and creative direction
- Low COGS, high perceived value, strong social share moment

### Booklet Aesthetic

Booklets use a mix of official institutional documents (forms, intake paperwork, memos) and handwritten/annotated notes from the OG character. The official documents provide the straight man; the handwriting provides the punchline. This contrast is essential — neither works alone.

Official elements: typed forms, bureaucratic headers, case numbers, checkboxes, signature lines, institutional seals. Handwritten elements: marginalia, corrections, crossed-out text, arrows, underlines, sticky notes, doodles. The overall impression is a real document that someone has lived with, marked up, and passed on.

## 7. Tray Specifications

### Tray Design Principles (Both Trays)

- **Die-cut recesses (Pelican case style):** Each item sits in a precisely shaped molded cutout — not an open tray with loose items. Each recess has a chalk-outline silhouette printed on the tray surface surrounding the cutout, plus the item name and a short tagline printed adjacent to the recess. This provides a premium unboxing feel and prevents all item shifting during shipping.
- Copy on tray surfaces includes item names and short taglines next to each die-cut recess — the extra inch of case length (14" vs. 13") provides space for this copy alongside the cutouts.
- Story lives in the booklets; trays are the payoff, not the explanation. Tray copy is labels and taglines, not paragraphs.
- Each tray lifts out independently via hybrid lift tabs (see Section 9).
- Tray material: rigid paperboard or molded pulp, matching interior color scheme.
- Trays are full interior width of the front zone — no ring clearance needed.

*For all tray dimensions (exterior, interior, wall heights, floor thickness), see Physical Dimensions & Sourcing Spec.*
