## 1. Concept: The Contraband Manual

The packaging is a makeshift briefcase — an improvised survival manual assembled on the inside and passed down. Not issued from above. Built by someone who survived and wanted to make sure you do too. This is the OG character's artifact.

The contraband manual aesthetic (binder-clipped document sets, handmade feel) is a character in the world — it reinforces the duct tape handle, the matte black exterior, the faux clasps. Everything about this object says: *someone made this under difficult conditions because they needed it.*

The exterior reads as a worn, institutional briefcase. The interior reveals a two-zone contraband layout — a front zone with stacked item trays separated by binder-clipped document sets, and a back zone hiding the final payoff in a padded compartment. Each layer builds a narrative arc from intake to release. The recipient doesn't just open a box. They get processed.

| Accent color | Minimal — limited to printed elements (chalk outlines in white/cream, copy in white or cream, orange accents) |

### Chalk Outline & Die-Cut Recess Style

Every item in every tray sits within a die-cut recess (Pelican case style) — a molded cutout shaped precisely to each item. This is not an open tray with loose items; each item has a fitted pocket that prevents shifting and provides a premium unboxing feel.

Each die-cut recess has a chalk-outline silhouette printed on the tray surface surrounding the cutout, plus the item name and a short tagline printed adjacent to the recess. The chalk outlines serve three purposes: they tell the recipient where each item goes (and that one is "missing" if removed), they reinforce the crime-scene / institutional world, and they provide a clean, premium presentation that reads as intentional rather than cluttered.

**Exception: Confiscated Stapler Recess.** One recess in Tray 2 is intentionally EMPTY — a small stapler silhouette with a chalk outline but no item. The label reads: "CONFISCATED — WEAPONIZED OFFICE EQUIPMENT. INCIDENT #147. DO NOT REISSUE." The absence of the item is the punchline. See Section 7 for details.

Outlines are printed in white or cream on the darker tray floor. Line weight should feel hand-drawn — not perfectly uniform, not digitally precise. Slightly rough edges. Think actual chalk on a dark surface, not vector art.

The interior is divided into two zones by a full-height divider wall:

**Front Zone (facing the person):** Trays 1 and 2 stacked vertically with binder-clipped document sets between them. Trays are full interior width (no ring clearance needed) and centered in the front zone.

**Back Zone (along back wall):** A hidden padded compartment containing the Mug Shot mug (on its side, handle to the side) and Corporate Parole Passport nestled in shredded "CLASSIFIED" paper padding. Completely hidden while trays are in place. Revealed when Tray 2 is lifted and a lift-off cover is removed.

## 6. Binder-Clipped Document Set System

### Overview

The narrative is delivered through two binder-clipped document sets that sit on top of each tray. These replace the coil-bound booklet system from v6. The document sets are standalone objects — removable, readable, keepable. They preserve and enhance the contraband manual feel: a stack of mixed-media documents held together by an oversized black binder clip reads as something a survivor actually assembled, not something manufactured.

The binder clip IS the binding — no coils, no staples, no saddle-stitch. The black oversized binder clip reads as intentional, not cheap — it's an office supply being used in-world. This is MORE authentic to the "survivor assembled this" narrative than manufactured coil-bound booklets.

### Document Set Design Specs

| Attribute | Spec |
|-----------|------|
| Binding | Single oversized black binder clip per set — the clip IS the binding mechanism |
| Belly band | Printed paper wrap around each set labeled "CASE FILE #XX — CLASSIFIED" — slides off before reading |
| Official documents | Heavy card stock (100–120lb cover) — intake forms, incident reports, memos, institutional paperwork |
| Handwritten pages | Lighter-weight stock (60–80lb text) — OG character's handwritten notes, annotations, survival tips |
| Photo prints | Polaroid-style printed cards — white border, slight aging, inserted within the stack |
| Finish | Uncoated or matte — writable surface, institutional paper feel |
| Interior page color | Off-white / cream for official documents — NOT bright white. Aged, institutional. |

*For all document set dimensions and thicknesses, see Physical Dimensions & Sourcing Spec.*

### Document Set Titles

Each set's belly band serves as the title: "CASE FILE #01 — CLASSIFIED" and "CASE FILE #02 — CLASSIFIED." The belly band slides off to reveal the document stack beneath.

### WARNING / DOCUMENTED CASES (Document Set 1, First Page)

The first page of Document Set 1 contains a WARNING notice. This sets the tone immediately — bureaucratic language played completely straight, establishing that this "manual" has a documented history of incidents.

Content direction: A formal warning that this survival kit has been associated with documented workplace incidents. Lists 3–5 absurd "cases" in official language. Establishes that this manual is contraband — possession is not officially sanctioned. Tone: dead serious. Format: institutional warning notice.

*Full copy lives in the Copy Document.*

### Sticky Notes (Inside Both Document Sets)

- Attached to pages within each set — not loose, not in pockets
- Electronics-grade adhesive — stays put through shipping and handling
- Designed to look like sticky notes — color, shape, lined or unlined
- Content: OG character's handwritten annotations, warnings, survival tips
- These are the primary mechanism for the OG character's voice showing up throughout the pages
- The handwriting is what makes it feel personal, contraband, passed-down

### Polaroid Cards (Split Across Both Document Sets)

- Polaroid-style printed cards — white border, slight aging
- Split across both document sets (some in Set 1, some in Set 2)
- Inserted within the document stack as part of the mixed-media contents
- Content TBD pending OG character details and creative direction
- Low COGS, high perceived value, strong social share moment

### Document Set Aesthetic

Document sets use a mix of official institutional documents (forms, intake paperwork, memos) printed on heavy card stock and handwritten/annotated notes from the OG character on lighter-weight paper. The official documents provide the straight man; the handwriting provides the punchline. This contrast is essential — neither works alone.

The variety of textures (heavy card stock, lighter paper, polaroid-style photo prints, the binder clip itself) creates a curated, premium feel — like someone actually assembled this collection over time. This tactile variety is a key advantage over uniform coil-bound booklets.

Official elements: typed forms, bureaucratic headers, case numbers, checkboxes, signature lines, institutional seals. Handwritten elements: marginalia, corrections, crossed-out text, arrows, underlines, sticky notes, doodles. The overall impression is a real document collection that someone has lived with, marked up, and passed on.

## 7. Tray Specifications

### Tray Design Principles (Both Trays)

- **Die-cut recesses (Pelican case style):** Each item sits in a precisely shaped molded cutout — not an open tray with loose items. Each recess has a chalk-outline silhouette printed on the tray surface surrounding the cutout, plus the item name and a short tagline printed adjacent to the recess. This provides a premium unboxing feel and prevents all item shifting during shipping.
- Copy on tray surfaces includes item names and short taglines next to each die-cut recess — the extra inch of case length (14" vs. 13") provides space for this copy alongside the cutouts.
- Story lives in the document sets; trays are the payoff, not the explanation. Tray copy is labels and taglines, not paragraphs.
- Each tray lifts out independently via hybrid lift tabs (see Section 9).
- Tray material: rigid paperboard or molded pulp, matching interior color scheme.
- Trays are full interior width of the front zone — no ring clearance needed.

## 11. Copy Surfaces — Each Zone

### Front Zone — Tray Copy Surfaces

Each tray has multiple surfaces that can carry printed copy or design elements. The key distinction is between surfaces visible during unboxing and surfaces only visible once items are removed.

**Visible Surfaces (seen during unboxing):**

| Surface | Description | Copy Approach |
|---------|-------------|---------------|
| Tray top (items in die-cut recesses) | Chalk outlines + item names + short taglines next to each recess | Names and taglines — not paragraphs. Extra case length (14") provides room. Includes evidence bag label, confiscated stapler label. |
| Tray walls (interior sides) | Visible when looking down into tray | Available for short copy — institutional markings, case numbers, evidence labels |
| Lift tabs (back-edge) | Decorative tabs, visible before tray is lifted | Single word or short phrase |

**Hidden Surfaces (revealed as items are removed or trays are lifted):**

| Surface | Description | Copy Approach |
|---------|-------------|---------------|
| Tray wall interiors | Inside faces of tray walls — visible only once items are lifted out of recesses | Easter egg copy — short OG character messages, warnings, or jokes. Discovered by the curious. Zero additional COGS since trays are already being printed. |
| Tray bottom (underside) | Revealed when tray is lifted out | Available for hidden copy — Easter eggs, jokes, messages that reward exploration |
| Inside the mug | Small printed card or tag placed inside the mug | Final easter egg in the entire kit — discovered only by people who look inside. "You looked inside. Smart. That instinct will keep you alive in here." |
| Booking frame back | Reverse side of booking frame — visible when recipient flips it over after photo moment | Comedy text, institutional fine print, or booking instructions. Zero additional COGS — already printing the front. |

**Design principle:** Hidden surfaces are bonus moments. Not everyone will look inside a tray wall or flip a tray over, but those who do should be rewarded. One good line per surface. Keep it short. These are whispered jokes, not shouted ones. The mug note is the LAST easter egg — the final reward for the most curious unboxer.
