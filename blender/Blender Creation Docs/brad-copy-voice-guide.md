# Brad Sterling — Copy Voice Guide
## For Writing Exterior Packaging & Brad-Authored Interior Elements

---

## THE TWO BRADS

Brad operates in two distinct modes, and the comedy comes from the fact that they exist on the same box, written by the same person, with zero self-awareness about the gap between them.

### Brad Mode 1: Product Copy Brad
**When Brad is writing about the office, the product, the jokes**

This Brad is actually competent. He knows his audience. He can write a sardonic Nutrition Facts label, a deadpan IT Asset Tag, a perfectly absurd Care & Storage warning. This is Brad the marketer — he understands what's funny about office life, and he packages it well. This is why Elevate Co. exists at all. The product works.

**Voice:** Dry, institutional, straight-faced. The humor comes from the format (official labels, FDA layouts, QC stickers) played totally straight while the content escalates into absurdity. Brad didn't invent this tone — he lifted it from OG's original kit — but he executes it competently.

**Examples (from existing exterior copy):**
- "PROPERTY OF: NOBODY ANYMORE"
- "Serving Size: 1 Monday / Servings Per Container: Too Many"
- "DO NOT REMOVE UNDER PENALTY OF MONDAY"
- "Not a significant source of work-life balance, upward mobility, or the feeling that any of this matters."

**Why it works:** The reader laughs WITH the copy. It's genuinely funny. Brad knows how to sell a gag gift.

---

### Brad Mode 2: Personal Brand Brad
**When Brad is writing about Brad**

This Brad is a disaster. The moment the subject becomes himself — his story, his vision, his credentials, his photo — every ounce of self-awareness evaporates. He becomes a hustle-culture LinkedIn post given physical form. He is completely, unshakably sincere. He never winks. He thinks this is what leadership looks like.

**Voice:** Aspirational, self-mythologizing, motivational-speaker cadence. Sentences that sound like they belong in a keynote address at a conference with 30 attendees. Vocabulary borrowed from startup culture, TED Talks, and lifestyle brand copy. Everything is "premium," "vision," "disrupt," "elevate."

**Examples:**
- "He didn't see contraband. He saw potential."
- "Premium workplace tools, premium packaging, and a premium price point."
- "Three-time nominee for the International Workplace Innovation Awards and an occasional keynote speaker at conferences he organizes himself."
- "A Brad Sterling Experience™"

**Why it works:** The reader laughs AT Brad. The comedy is that this man wrote a perfectly good Nutrition Facts joke on one side of the box and this delusional founder bio on the other, and he sees absolutely no contradiction.

---

## THE GAP IS THE JOKE

The single funniest thing about the exterior packaging is that both Brads are on the same box. The reader encounters competent product humor AND unhinged self-promotion, realizes the same person created both, and that realization is its own punchline.

This means: **don't sabotage the product copy to make Brad look dumber.** The Nutrition Facts label should be genuinely great. The IT Asset Tag should land. The sardonic institutional voice should work. Because the BETTER the product copy is, the MORE absurd Brad's founder bio becomes by contrast. If everything on the box were bad, Brad would just be incompetent. The fact that he's good at the job and terrible at self-awareness is what makes him a real (and funnier) character.

## BRAD'S COPY VOICE: PRACTICAL REFERENCE

### Product Copy (Mode 1) — How to Write It

**Format is the straight man. Content is the comedian.**
Take a real format (nutrition label, asset tag, QC sticker, care instructions, evidence tag) and fill it with escalating office absurdity. The format never breaks. The content gets progressively more unhinged.

**Escalation structure:**
1. First line reads as completely plausible
2. Second line has a slight edge
3. Third line is clearly a joke but the format stays serious
4. Final line is full absurd but still formatted perfectly

**Example — IT Asset Tag:**
> ASSET TAG: #000001 *(plausible)*
> PROPERTY OF: NOBODY ANYMORE *(edge)*
> DEPT: N/A *(dry)*
> STATUS: DEPLOYED *(still plausible)*
> DO NOT REMOVE UNDER PENALTY OF MONDAY *(full absurd, format intact)*

**Word choice:**
- Institutional vocabulary: "deployed," "authorized," "issued," "designated"
- Bureaucratic precision: case numbers, dates, serial numbers, item codes
- Office-specific pain points named with clinical detachment
- "Kevin" as a recurring proper noun treated as a known hazard

**What to avoid:**
- Puns (too cute — Brad's product copy is dry, not clever)
- Exclamation points (the format is always flat and institutional)
- Breaking the format (the form never acknowledges it's a joke)
- Winking at the reader (the straight face IS the technique)

---

### Personal Brand Copy (Mode 2) — How to Write It

**Brad is writing his own legend and he believes every word.**

The key is sincerity. Brad is not trying to be funny. Brad is trying to be inspiring. Everything he writes about himself is what he genuinely believes a visionary founder sounds like, informed by every LinkedIn thought-leader post, every startup origin story, every Gary Vee clip he's ever absorbed.

**Sentence structures Brad loves:**
- Origin myth framing: "When [Brad] discovered [thing], he didn't see [obvious interpretation]. He saw [grandiose reinterpretation]."
- Pivot-as-epiphany: "That's when everything changed."
- The false-humble brag: "Brad is an occasional keynote speaker at conferences he organizes himself." (Brad wrote this line. Brad thinks this is impressive.)
- Premium stacking: Three adjectives minimum. "Premium workplace tools, premium packaging, and a premium price point." Brad genuinely thinks saying "premium" three times makes something premium.
- The credential drop: Awards that sound almost real. Organizations that might exist. Titles that are definitely self-given.

**Word choice:**
- "Vision," "disrupt," "elevate," "curate," "experience"
- "Whitespace," "thought leader," "brand," "premium"
- "Professional Edition" (Brad thinks adding this to anything makes it luxury)
- His own name (Brad refers to Brad in the third person in marketing copy)

**What to avoid:**
- Making Brad aware of the joke (he never is)
- Having Brad be mean or cynical (he's earnest, not sarcastic — his product copy is sarcastic, but HIS BRAND is dead serious)
- Going too far into parody (Brad should feel like a real person you've met, not a cartoon)

---

### The Credentials Escalation Pattern

Brad's credentials are a comedy goldmine. They follow a specific pattern: they start almost-credible and slide into obviously self-generated, but Brad presents them all with the same level of pride.

**The pattern:**
1. Real-sounding title: "Founder & CEO, Elevate Co." *(Sure, okay)*
2. Almost-real award: "Three-time nominee for the International Workplace Innovation Awards" *(Hmm... is that real?)*
3. Self-undermining detail played straight: "...and an occasional keynote speaker at conferences he organizes himself" *(There it is)*

**More credential-style lines to pull from:**
- "Featured in [publication that might be his own blog]"
- "Host of [podcast name], a top voice in workplace disruption" (11 listeners, 9 are bots)
- "Named one of [city]'s Most Innovative Founders Under 50 by [award he created]"
- "Author of the forthcoming [book title that will never be written]"
- "Brad has been described as 'relentless' — Brad Sterling, describing himself"

## OG BLEED-THROUGH FRAMEWORK

### The Concept
OG got to this specific copy of the kit before it shipped. OG's voice appears as infiltration — handwritten notes, sticky notes, corrections, annotations on top of Brad's polished work. This is an act of reclamation: *this is still mine.*

### How OG Shows Up on Brad's Exterior

OG's bleed-through on the exterior should feel like someone got to the box with a pen AFTER Brad's design was printed. These are not designed elements — they're interventions.

**Mechanisms:**
- Handwritten text appearing in margins or blank spaces on exterior labels
- Sticky notes placed on top of printed elements
- Words circled with handwritten commentary
- Arrows pointing to Brad's copy with corrections
- Small doodles in OG's stick-figure style tucked into corners

**Frequency on exterior:**
Sparse. The exterior is Brad's domain. OG's presence should feel like it snuck in — 2 to 4 small interventions across the whole exterior, not a takeover. Enough that an observant reader notices and grins. Not so much that it overwhelms Brad's design.

**Tone of OG's exterior interventions:**
- Not angry. More like a weary eye-roll from someone who's seen it all.
- Could range from dry corrections ("*he found it in a break room in Akron*") to resigned commentary ("*...he charges $49.99 for this*") to territorial marks ("*still mine*").
- OG's humor is drier and more understated than Brad's product copy. OG doesn't escalate — OG deadpans.

### How OG Shows Up on Brad's Interior Elements

Inside the kit, wherever Brad added his own branded inserts or relabeled OG's items, OG's bleed-through can be more aggressive. This is OG's territory — the interior is where the real kit lives.

**Mechanisms:**
- Sticky notes on Brad's branded inserts with corrections or mockery
- Handwritten original item names next to Brad's relabeled versions
- OG crossing out Brad's copy and writing the real version
- OG's annotations on Brad's instructions ("*this is not how you use this*")
- Small handwritten notes that hint at the original kit's history

**Frequency on interior:**
More generous than exterior. Every Brad-authored interior element should have at least one OG intervention. The reader should feel OG's growing irritation (or amusement) as they encounter Brad's additions.

## WRITING BRAD'S "ABOUT THE FOUNDER" PANEL

This is the single most important Brad element. It's where both Brads collide — the guy who made a pretty good gag gift AND the guy who thinks he's a visionary. Guidelines:

### Structure
1. **Origin myth** (2-3 sentences): How Brad "discovered" the kit. Frame it as Brad's epiphany moment. He uses language that makes finding someone else's work sound like innovation.
2. **Vision statement** (1-2 sentences): What Brad built from it. Premium, premium, premium.
3. **Credentials** (1-2 sentences): The escalating-absurdity credential stack. Starts almost-real, ends obviously self-generated.
4. **Optional tagline or sign-off**: Something that makes it clear this is a Brad Sterling Production.

### Photo Direction
- Brad art-directed this photo himself
- Big cheesy teeth-too-white smile — this is his "I'm winning you over" face
- Purple satin shirt, oversized gold rope chain (comically large — think 80s rapper), aviators, pinky ring
- The lighting, framing, and styling are what Brad genuinely thinks looks powerful
- Gold frame or premium border treatment — Brad chose this

### Tone
100% sincere. Zero irony. Brad is proud of this panel. He thinks it elevates the product. He showed it to people before printing and they all said "wow, Brad" and he took that as a compliment.

## THE COMEDY HIERARCHY

When writing any piece of exterior copy, the comedy should come from these sources, in this order:

1. **The format/content contrast** — official format, absurd content (this is the backbone)
2. **Brad's self-image vs. reality** — most concentrated in the founder panel but present in any "premium" branding language
3. **OG's quiet presence** — the handwritten interventions that signal to the reader: *someone real made the thing inside this box*
4. **The cumulative effect** — by the time the reader has seen all the exterior elements, the density of institutional parody labels on a matte black briefcase with a duct tape handle is itself the joke. It's too much. It's perfect. It's Brad.
